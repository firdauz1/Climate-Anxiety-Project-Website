The Invisible Costs of AI

AI feels weightless, but its impacts are physical.

Data centers are often discussed through technical language: megawatts, cooling systems, permits, and zoning. But for communities, these impacts can feel personal. Questions about water use, energy demand, land use, air quality, and public transparency can generate feelings of anxiety, uncertainty, distrust, and concern about the future. Rather than being obstacles to rational decision-making, these emotions can help explain why communities seek information, attend public meetings, organize, and advocate for accountability. By making these concerns visible, this project explores how understanding emotional responses can help transform uncertainty into informed engagement and collective action.

Water

Cooling systems and industrial growth can raise questions about water demand, especially in dry regions.

Energy

Large data centers can increase electricity demand and raise concerns about grid strain and utility costs.

Backup generators or gas turbines can create pollution concerns near already burdened communities.

🌾

Land Use

Large campuses can transform farmland, open space, and areas near homes, schools, parks, or public institutions.

👁️

Transparency

Many conflicts are about whether residents had enough information, time, and public input.

Featured Case Study

Stratos: When AI Arrives in a Community

The Stratos project in Box Elder County, Utah became a central example of how AI infrastructure can generate environmental concern and public backlash. Residents and advocacy groups raised questions about water, land use, energy demand, transparency, and who gets to participate in decisions that shape the future of a place.

💧 Water

Community concerns focused on whether large-scale data infrastructure could place additional pressure on water resources in an already sensitive region.

⚡ Energy

The project’s proposed scale raised questions about the energy required to power AI infrastructure and who would bear the costs.

👥 Public Process

Residents questioned whether the approval process gave communities enough information, time, and influence.

From Anxiety to Action

Communities do not have to respond to data center proposals alone. Public meetings, zoning hearings, environmental reviews, petitions, and direct contact with representatives can all become ways to transform anxiety into accountability.

1. Check local agendas

Look for city council, county commission, planning commission, and zoning board meetings.

2. Ask for documents

Request environmental reviews, water-rights applications, power agreements, traffic studies, and zoning files.

3. Submit public comments

Written comments create a public record and can influence permitting and approval decisions.

4. Contact representatives

State and local officials may influence zoning, utility regulation, environmental review, and public transparency.

Reflection: What the maps cannot show alone

The maps show where data centers exist and where communities have publicly responded, but they cannot fully capture what it feels like to live near a proposed project or to learn that invisible digital systems depend on visible environmental tradeoffs. This is where climate anxiety becomes more than an individual feeling. It becomes a response to uncertainty, lack of transparency, and the fear that decisions about water, land, energy, and health are being made without meaningful public participation.

By combining data, community responses, direct voices, and action resources, this project argues that anxiety can become a starting point for accountability. The goal is not only to make AI infrastructure visible, but to help people ask who benefits, who bears the risks, and how communities can participate in shaping the future of the technologies they are asked to live with.
